Chemical Engineering in the Uranium Mining industry

How Uranium Gets Mined

 Uranium is found as an ore underground, where pipes are dug around it. These pipes remove the heat from the ground, freezing both the uranium and any ground water which could interfere. 200 pipes are used to surround the ore, which are then filled with cooled calcium chloride to remove the heat.

 After this is setup, the actual mining starts. A pilot hole is dug into the ground, which will map the drill’s path and creates an entrypoint for the larger mining equipment. The reamer bit, the first picture on the right, drills the uranium, causing it to fall. A trolly then scoops it up, and the ore gets measured to determine its uranium density. Subsequenty, the ore gets powdered, then turned into a slurry to be sent to the mill.

 The mill dissolves just the uranium in acid, then applies multiple chemical processes to purify the uranium. Next, the uranium gets roasted, then gets turned into uranium tri-oxide, pictured as the jar labeled "UO3" on the right. The powder then gets converted into uranium dioxide, which is the chemical arrangement required for nuclear fission. The next step is to compress the powder into pellets, then baked again to increase their density. Lastly, zirconium fuel tubes are attached and capped to the pellets, which makes it a uranium fuel rod.

Roles for Chemical Engineers

Metallurgist

Responsibility

 The responsibility of metallurgists is to purify the uranium metal from ore. They design, monitor, and control the entire process, and are responsible for testing the outputs and ensuring certain quality standards. In addition, they must meet safety standards.
